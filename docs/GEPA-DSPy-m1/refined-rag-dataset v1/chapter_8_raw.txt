[CHUNK 01]
CONCEPT: The Formula for Guilt — G = W1 + W2
TYPE: formula
REFERENCES: The ABCs of How Emotions are Created (Chapter 3); IB4: Shoulds, Musts, and Demands (Chapter 4); IB5: Rating (Chapter 4)

Even though we all feel guilty about different things, we all create Guilt in the exact same way:
G = W1 + W2
Guilt = Wrongness + Worthlessness
I call Guilt the bottom line of the four blocks because it takes rejection to a whole new level with the rejection of the 'Self.' In fact, you can feel guilty about having Depression, you can feel Guilty about having Anxiety, you can feel Guilty about having Anger problems, and you can even feel Guilty about feeling Guilty!

[CHUNK 02]
CONCEPT: W1: Wrongness — Definition
TYPE: definition
REFERENCES: The Formula for Guilt — G = W1 + W2; IB4: Shoulds, Musts, and Demands (Chapter 4); FHB — Fallible Human Beings (Chapter 6)

Wrongness is the idea that you have done something wrong, that some action you have taken, or some thought that has passed through your mind was wrong and bad and you should feel Guilty about it. There is a Should component in Wrongness too of course (always look for the Should). It is not the event itself that causes your Guilt. It is the 'Should' you attach to the event that makes you feel Guilty. If you did something wrong and stopped right there: 'I did something wrong—period! I simply made a mistake,' you would never feel Guilty! It is only when we believe that we should be perfect, that we should never make mistakes, and that when we do, we are worth less as a person, as a human being, that we feel Guilt! Remember that no one is a PHB, a Perfect Human Being—we are all FHBs, Fallible Human Beings. Wrongness is an absolute, so something can't be defined as wrong unless it is wrong for everyone, thus very few things can truly be defined as wrong. Wrong exists as a belief and not a fact.

[CHUNK 03]
CONCEPT: W2: Worthlessness — Definition
TYPE: definition
REFERENCES: The Formula for Guilt — G = W1 + W2; IB5: Rating (Chapter 4); W1: Wrongness

Although I'd like to blame everything about Guilt on the word 'Should,' Guilt, like the other stumbling blocks, has a rating component. This brings us to worthlessness, the W2. If you are feeling Guilty, you are telling yourself that you should have done something differently, and because you did not, you are somehow inferior. The culmination of both of these irrational beliefs is, '…and because of this, I am a bad person (my essence is badness)! I have failed at some or many of my ventures, therefore I must be a failure!' Worth is not a real attribute. You cannot touch it and you cannot measure it, so worth does not exist except in your own mind. So if worth doesn't rationally exist, how can you be worth anything? And if you can't be worth anything to start with, then how can you be worth less? This is rational thinking! Freedom from constantly comparing ourselves to others and having to 'prove our worth.'

[CHUNK 04]
CONCEPT: The BS Equation — Behavior = Self
TYPE: framework
REFERENCES: W2: Worthlessness; IB5: Rating (Chapter 4); The Formula for Guilt — G = W1 + W2

When we believe that our mistakes damage our self-worth, it's because we've bought into the B=S equation: Behavior=Self. This equation states that what we did equals who we are—wholly and permanently! This isn't true, so it's an irrational belief. Behavior and self are not equal or interchangeable.
The biggest BS we tell ourselves is that our behavior equals our self and self-worth:
Behavior=Self
But this is incorrect. Behavior does not equal self.
B≠S
Behavior only equals behavior:
B=B
It is rational to rate behavior not only because rating behavior can help us see what change is appropriate to solve our practical problems, but more importantly because behavior leads to the action necessary to achieve those solutions. It is factual and provable. If, however, I transfer the properties of my behavior to myself, I'm no longer rating the behavior, I'm now rating myself—wholly and permanently. When you catch yourself feeling Guilty remember that it means you're full of B=S—the BS equation, that is—and realize that your behavior does not and cannot equal your Self.

[CHUNK 05]
CONCEPT: Unconditional Self-Acceptance — Definition
TYPE: definition
REFERENCES: W2: Worthlessness; The BS Equation; IB5: The Golden Rule of Rational Living (Chapter 4)

Unconditional self-acceptance means that you accept yourself because you are alive and have decided to accept yourself. It does not mean self-esteem, self-confidence, self-respect, or self-regard. For all these terms imply that you can only accept yourself because you do something well or because other people like you. Unconditional self-acceptance means you accept yourself because you are alive and have decided to accept yourself. Only a limited number of talented, intelligent, competent, and well-loved people can gain self-esteem or self-confidence. But anyone, merely because he or she chooses to have it, can gain self-acceptance. Worthwhileness is almost as dangerous as its counterpart worthlessness. In fact, just as soon as you tend to think in terms of a personal 'worth' you almost automatically tend to think in terms of personal 'worthlessness.' You'll be much better off with the concept that you are not 'worthwhile' because of your effectiveness nor 'worthless' because of your ineffectiveness. You merely exist.

[CHUNK 06]
CONCEPT: Procrastination — Identification, Understanding, and Overcoming
TYPE: framework
REFERENCES: The Formula for Guilt — G = W1 + W2; IB2: Awfulizing (Chapter 4); The BS Equation; FHB — Fallible Human Beings (Chapter 6)

No discussion of Guilt would be complete without a good look at procrastination, because many people feel guilty while procrastinating.

REASONS FOR PROCRASTINATION:
• Fear of the Future: You are afraid of failing. If you pushed ahead and completed the task, it may not turn out as well as you thought it would. As long as you don't attempt to do a task, you don't have the possibility of failure.
• Awfulizing: You exaggerate the consequences of a less-than-perfect outcome. You tell yourself it would be awful if you failed and that you couldn't stand it, so you put it off.
• Unrealistic Expectations: You think you can do much more than you can. You pile up tasks and then find yourself unable to complete them. Eventually you feel overwhelmed and put off doing anything at all.
• Approval: You're afraid to say no. You are afraid you may lose other people's approval which you think you need in order to approve of yourself.

WAYS WE KEEP PROCRASTINATING ALIVE:
• Rationalizing: You tell yourself things that in your mind justify the delay: 'I'll start tomorrow,' 'I'm tired and as soon as I've rested, I'll start,' 'I always do my best work at the eleventh hour.' More B=S.
• Impulsivity: You give up because the task seems too hard. Then, from time to time, you burst with energy and work hard for the quick kill, which doesn't come.
• Escapism: Instead of doing what needs to be done, you believe a magical solution will present itself if you just wait and are patient. You substitute fantasy for reality.

OVERCOMING PROCRASTINATION:
• Recognize that it is what you are telling yourself that defeats your efforts.
• Begin now. Stop believing you need inspiration before you start. Getting into a task usually brings inspiration.
• Accept that you are human and will find it difficult to accomplish hard tasks. Set small goals and reward yourself when you accomplish them.
• Write yourself reminders and place them where you will see them every day.
• Know that you can change. Behavior is still just behavior (B=B), and can be changed with effort. You are not stuck with your procrastination for life.

[CHUNK 07]
CONCEPT: Case Example — Divorced Mother (Guilt Formula with 4 Steps)
TYPE: example
REFERENCES: The Formula for Guilt — G = W1 + W2; The 4 Steps to Disputing (Chapter 4); The BS Equation

A patient felt terribly guilty over getting divorced, particularly for her daughter who now had to be raised in a 'broken' home. Applying The 4 Steps to Disputing:
STEP 1: OBSERVE — This woman was feeling extreme Guilt, so she was buying her daughter everything she wanted to compensate for the perceived lack of love.
STEP 2: SOS — The patient believed the concept that she was wrong for getting a divorce, that she was a poor excuse for a mother (Self) for not providing a traditional nuclear family for her daughter (Others), and that she was worth less as a woman and as a human being because she wasn't able to keep her marriage together (Situation).
STEP 3: IDENTIFY — 'Why is it that my daughter must be raised by her biological parents, and that they stay married to each other? Where in the universe is it written that every child needs to have their every desire satisfied, at all times, and they must only be raised by their biological parents? Is it really Awful that you are no longer married to your first husband?'
STEP 4: REPLACE — 'It's unfortunate that my marriage didn't work out, but I can still enjoy being a mom.' 'I feel frustrated that my life hasn't worked out exactly how I thought it would.' 'I want my daughter to have a great childhood but giving her presents won't guarantee her permanent happiness and will not make up for my divorcing her father.'
